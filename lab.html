A Lab at the Intersection

The shape and principles of the research group taking form, where fluid physics, microfluidic engineering, biological matter, and computational experiment meet.

Identity

A Lab at the Intersection

A research group at the boundary between fluid physics, microfluidic engineering, biological matter, and computational experiment. We build our own instruments, design our own chips, write our own analysis software, and publish work that is both scientifically rigorous and visually compelling.

Core themes: evaporation-driven self-assembly, microfluidic platforms for biological systems, soft matter and biomolecular condensates, AI-assisted experimental analysis, and visual tools for understanding complex flow systems.

Lab Principles

Build Before You Measure

Every member of the group will learn to design and fabricate experimental setups. Understanding the instrument is inseparable from understanding the measurement.

Visual Rigor

Data visualization, microscopy, and figure design are not afterthoughts. They are part of the scientific method. The aesthetics of data are taken as seriously as the statistics.

Interdisciplinary by Default

The problems we study require physics, engineering, biology, and computation. The group will reflect that range. Everyone learns enough of each other's language to collaborate meaningfully.

Open and Reproducible

Code, designs, protocols, and data will be shared openly whenever possible. Reproducibility is not a burden. It is a standard of quality.

What the Lab Is Doing

A research program already in motion across four threads:

Build Designing and fabricating microfluidic chips, valve manifolds, motion stages, and the control software that makes them experimental instruments.
Measure Imaging cells, droplets, and condensates in real time. Combining brightfield, fluorescence, and PIV with custom analysis pipelines.
Model CFD, transport modeling, and statistical inference to connect what we measure to physical mechanism.
Share Publishing methods, releasing code, mentoring students, and writing about the work in language that travels across disciplines.

Interested?

If this resonates with the kind of science you want to do, reach out. Whether you are a student, a collaborator, or a future colleague, the work is open to people who share its principles.
